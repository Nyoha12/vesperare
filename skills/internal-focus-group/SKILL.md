# internal-focus-group

## When to use

Use this skill when an idea, interface, project direction, output, or prototype should be tested against likely users, audiences, performers, readers, or listeners.

Use it to clarify:
- confusion points;
- desirability;
- usability;
- emotional response;
- mismatch with audience;
- practical objections.

## When not to use

Do not use it as a replacement for real feedback.

Do not use it if the target audience is undefined.

Do not use generic personas when specific audience knowledge exists.

Do not use it to decide final artistic value.

## Inputs

- idea or output to test;
- target audience;
- audience context;
- constraints;
- expected reaction;
- known audience preferences;
- decision to inform.

## Steps

1. Define the target audience.
2. Define anti-audience if relevant.
3. Create 3–7 audience lenses.
4. For each lens, evaluate independently:
   - what they understand;
   - what confuses them;
   - what attracts them;
   - what repels them;
   - what they would need next;
   - what risk the idea creates.
5. Synthesize patterns.
6. Separate simulated feedback from real evidence.
7. Identify what should be tested with real humans.
8. Return a recommendation.

```md
## Human decision required
```

## Verification

The focus group is acceptable if:

- the audience is explicit;
- the personas/lenses are not generic;
- simulated feedback is not treated as real evidence;
- objections are preserved;
- real feedback needs are identified.

## Gotchas

- If the audience is unclear, stop and clarify it.
- Do not make every audience reaction positive.
- Do not optimize away artistic specificity.
- Do not let simulated users replace real listeners, performers, or collaborators.
